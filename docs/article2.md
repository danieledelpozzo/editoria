# Disinformation, Politics, and Rhetoric: What Spanish Lawmakers Really Talk About

## Introduction

"Fake president," "fake investiture," accusations of lying hurled back and forth across the chamber: in Spanish politics, the vocabulary of disinformation has become a routine weapon of parliamentary combat. But does this rhetoric reflect a genuine political commitment to fighting disinformation, or is it mostly a tool for scoring points against opponents? A study just published in *Frontiers in Communication* set out to answer exactly that question, analyzing over a thousand speeches given in Spain's Congress of Deputies during the first year of the COVID-19 pandemic — a period marked by high polarization and a documented surge in misinformation.

## The study: a year of parliamentary speeches under the microscope

The paper, *"Disinformation in the Spanish public debate: an analysis of political speeches in the Congress of Deputies"* (Domínguez-García, Velasco-Molpeceres and Pérez-Curiel, *Frontiers in Communication*, 2024), applied quantitative and qualitative content analysis to 1,115 parliamentary interventions delivered between January 2020 and January 2021, all containing at least one of six keywords: disinformation, hoax, lie, false, fake, and post-truth.

The first finding is almost counter-intuitive: despite the infodemic triggered by COVID-19, disinformation as a topic occupies a marginal place on the Spanish political agenda, appearing only about 17 times per plenary session on average. More tellingly, politicians rarely use the actual word "disinformation" (just 5.6% of the studied expressions) or "post-truth" (a mere 0.6%). Instead, they overwhelmingly prefer blunter terms like "lie" (45.2%) and "false" (32.6%) — words used less to analyze a systemic phenomenon and more to attack a specific opponent's specific claim.

This distinction matters. The researchers found that "disinformation" and "post-truth" tend to be used to describe broad, structural problems affecting society as a whole, while "lie," "hoax," "false," and "fake" are almost always pointed at a rival politician's individual statement. In practice, this means Spanish lawmakers rarely engage with disinformation as a policy issue to be solved — only 4.6% of the studied speeches proposed any concrete measure against it. The vast majority (84.9%) used these terms purely to attack the opposing side.

The analysis also uncovered a partisan pattern. Conservative opposition parties (PP and Vox) used disinformation-related vocabulary most frequently, while parties with a more nationalist profile, such as the Basque PNV and Catalan ERC, were comparatively more likely to use these terms to reinforce their own ideological narrative rather than simply to attack rivals. Government ministers, meanwhile, were the group most likely to use the term "disinformation" itself and to frame it constructively — though this happened in only a small fraction of cases.

## Why this matters for journalism

For journalists and science communicators covering political discourse, this study offers a useful diagnostic tool: it shows that when politicians invoke "disinformation," the word is more often a rhetorical weapon aimed at short-term polarization than a sign of genuine engagement with the problem. Recognizing this pattern can help reporters distinguish between substantive policy debate and purely confrontational rhetoric — and can inform coverage of how democratic institutions actually respond (or fail to respond) to one of the defining challenges of the information age.
