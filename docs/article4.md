# Fighting Fire with Fire: Mapping How AI Is Being Used Against Disinformation
*Published July 7, 2026*
## Introduction

If artificial intelligence can be used to mass-produce disinformation, can it also be turned against it? Governments, tech companies, and civil society have bet heavily that it can, funding dozens of AI-powered fact-checking tools, detection systems, and verification platforms over the past several years. But with so many initiatives launched independently across different countries, a basic question has gone largely unanswered: who is actually doing what, and how do these efforts relate to one another? A new study published in *Frontiers in Political Science* takes on exactly that question, mapping the global landscape of AI-driven counter-disinformation projects for the first time.

## The study: tracing the web of counter-disinformation initiatives

The paper, *"The use of artificial intelligence in counter-disinformation: a world wide (web) mapping"* (Pilati and Venturini, *Frontiers in Political Science*, 2025), does not analyze how well these AI tools work, instead, it maps the network of organizations building them, using an approach called hyperlink network analysis. The idea is simple but powerful: websites tend to link to others that share their goals or worldview, so studying who links to whom reveals the hidden communities behind a field. The researchers identified 81 active websites of organizations using or developing AI against disinformation, then crawled and mapped nearly 400 hyperlinks connecting them.

The resulting map reveals three distinct clusters. The largest, dominated by European initiatives, is built around EU-funded Horizon research projects and public research institutes, often working hand-in-hand with public IT infrastructure providers. A second, smaller cluster sits in the United States and is centered on university research (institutions like MIT and Harvard feature prominently) and think tanks, largely funded by private tech companies rather than public grants. The third cluster consists of established fact-checking organizations — familiar names like Snopes, PolitiFact, and FullFact many of which have received direct funding from Google or partnered with Meta's fact-checking program.

Beyond geography, the study identifies a meaningful difference in strategy. European and American research initiatives tend to work "upstream": they build AI tools meant to improve the overall quality of the information environment before disinformation even spreads widely, for example, systems that help verify authentic content or detect deepfakes. Fact-checking organizations, by contrast, generally work "downstream," using AI to speed up the process of debunking claims after they have already gone viral. Despite this difference, the authors note one constant across all approaches: human oversight remains indispensable as none of the mapped initiatives treat AI as a fully autonomous solution.

The study also flags real limitations in the current ecosystem. Almost the entire mapped network is concentrated in Europe and North America, with only isolated exceptions such as Argentina's Chequeado representing the Global South. Civil society groups beyond fact-checkers, such as media watchdogs and human rights organizations, are also strikingly underrepresented, suggesting that many of these organizations have not yet built AI capacity into their work. Interestingly, the researchers also found that many now-defunct projects were American initiatives launched during the peak of "fake news" panic around 2016–2017, which had aimed for fully automated content moderation, an ambition that ran into ethical and practical limits.

## Why this matters for journalism

For journalists and communicators, this mapping offers a practical orientation to a fragmented field: it clarifies which organizations are building the tools reporters might rely on for verification, who is funding them, and where the gaps are particularly the near-total absence of AI counter-disinformation capacity outside Europe and North America. Understanding these dynamics can help newsrooms choose which tools to trust, and can inform advocacy for expanding this kind of infrastructure to regions currently left out of the conversation.
