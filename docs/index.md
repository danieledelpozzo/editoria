# Science & Disinformation Briefs

## A fast, reliable digest for journalists and science communicators

Disinformation moves faster than most newsrooms can verify it. This project is a small experiment in closing that gap: a running collection of short, accessible briefings that translate recent open access scientific research into material journalists, editors, and science communicators can actually use — in a news story, a newsletter, or a social media post.

Each brief follows the same idea: take one peer-reviewed, open access paper on a topic currently in the public debate, and turn it into a short piece with a clear journalistic angle, a plain-language summary of what the research actually found, and a direct link back to the original source, so nothing here has to be taken on faith.

## What's in this collection

- **[Article 1](article1.md)** — Can chatbots be weaponized to mass-produce disinformation? A look at an ACL 2024 study that put ten major language models to the test.
- **[Article 2](article2.md)** — When politicians talk about "disinformation," what do they actually mean? An analysis of a year of speeches in the Spanish Congress of Deputies.
- **[Article 3](article3.md)** — How does misinformation on social media undermine vaccination campaigns and other public health efforts, and what can be done about it?
- **[Article 4](article4.md)** — Fighting fire with fire: a global map of the organizations building AI tools to detect and counter disinformation.

## Why open access matters here

Every brief in this collection is built on a study that is freely available to read in full, with no paywall. That is a deliberate choice: a project about improving public understanding of trustworthy information should itself be built on sources anyone can go back and check.

## A living, updatable format

This site is built with MkDocs, so new briefs can be added quickly as new research and new debates emerge — on AI, climate, health, or any other topic where accurate, timely science communication makes a difference. Think of it less as a finished publication and more as a template: a repeatable workflow for turning credible research into content that is ready to publish, share, or adapt.
